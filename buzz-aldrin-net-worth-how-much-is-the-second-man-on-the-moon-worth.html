    Who is Buzz Aldrin?

    Buzz Aldrin was born Edwin Eugene Aldrin Jr. on January 20, 1930, in Montclair, New Jersey.

    His nickname “Buzz” came from his sister who mispronounced “brother” as “buzzer” when he was a child.

    He legally changed his name to Buzz Aldrin in 1988.

    Buzz Aldrin’s Early Life and Education

    Buzz Aldrin grew up in a military family.

    His father was a colonel in the U.S. Air Force and his mother was the daughter of an Army chaplain.

    He graduated from Montclair High School in 1947 and attended the U.S. Military Academy at West Point, where he graduated third in his class with a degree in mechanical engineering in 1951.

    Buzz then joined the Air Force as a fighter pilot.

    Buzz Aldrin’s Military Career

    Aldrin flew 66 combat missions in F-86 Sabre jets during the Korean War, shooting down two MiG-15 aircraft.

    He was awarded the Distinguished Flying Cross and three Air Medals for his service.

    After the war, he served as an aerial gunnery instructor and a flight commander in West Germany.

    He also attended the Squadron Officer School and the Aerospace Research Pilot School.

    In 1959, he enrolled at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) to pursue a doctorate in astronautics.

    His thesis topic was “Line-of-Sight Guidance Techniques for Manned Orbital Rendezvous,” which earned him the nickname “Dr. Rendezvous” from his fellow astronauts. He graduated with a Doctor of Science degree in 1963.

    Buzz Aldrin’s NASA Career and Apollo 11

    In October 1963, Buzz Aldrin was selected as one of the third group of NASA astronauts.

    He was the first astronaut with a doctoral degree and became an expert in docking and rendezvous techniques for spacecraft. He also pioneered underwater training methods to simulate spacewalking.

    His first spaceflight was aboard Gemini 12 in November 1966, where he performed three extravehicular activities (EVAs) totaling more than five hours.

    He set a new record for the longest spacewalk and demonstrated that humans could work effectively in space.

    His second and most famous spaceflight was aboard Apollo 11 in July 1969, where he became the lunar module pilot and the second person to walk on the moon after Neil Armstrong.

    He spent about two hours on the lunar surface with Armstrong, collecting rock samples, taking photographs, and setting up scientific equipment.

    Buzz also conducted the first religious ceremony on the moon by taking communion privately.

    The Apollo 11 mission was watched by an estimated 600 million people around the world and marked a historic achievement for humanity.
